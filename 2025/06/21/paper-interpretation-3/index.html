Paper-Interpretation-3-Transgenerational transmission of reproductive and metabolic dysfunction in the male progeny of polycystic ovary syndrome

This is the third paper’s interpretation of transgenerational epigenetic inheritance. This paper is about the reproductive and metabolic dysfunction that passed transgenerationally due to polycystic ovary syndrome. This paper, unlike the previous two, includes the observation and data collection on humans, which is rarely seen in the TEI research field as experiment or observation including humans across several generations is commonly considered costly and inconvenient.

In the paper “Transgenerational transmission of reproductive and metabolic dysfunction in the male progeny of polycystic ovary syndrome(Risal et al., 2023)”, the researchers use mice as models in replace of humans to verify the observation that the son of the polycystic ovary syndrome will increase the chance of getting obese. The researchers also verified that the small RNA expression change induces this transgenerational effect. At the same time, the effect is on the maternal side rather than the paternal side which is not similar to the previous two papers.

Research Background

Polycystic ovary syndrome is a disease that causes infertility in females and often induces other kinds of diseases like type 2 diabetes. The disease is commonly linked with syndromes like being overly obese which the researchers also observed in the offspring of the patients. Also, recent studies have found that the genetic factors for PCOS will also increase the risk of cardiovascular disease, and ß-cell dysfunction. A similar obesity syndrome is also observed across the generation as far as to F3 generation indicated by some researchers.

Experiment Object

In this experiment, the researchers want to determine the deep mechanism for the young age obesity of the child of a PCOS woman and also identify differentially expressed genes of small RNA through the GWAS associative analysis. Then the researchers will turn to the mice model by using prenatal androgen exposure to mimic the PCOS syndrome and showing that the obesity effect will be passed transgenerationally to F3 generation and which mechanism takes for this transgenerational effect.

Experiment Result

  1. The PCOS son will have an altered lipid circulation profile compared to the normal child. The data sample indicates the increased cholesterol concentration and circulation of low-density lipoprotein(LDL) for the PCOS sons. Also as the mother has a higher BMI which indicates obesity, the son of the obese mother will also have a higher BMI compared to the control group child with the mother’s BMI lower than 25.
  1. The researchers then used the sncRNA analysis of the serum of PCOS son identify miRNA targeted loci risk gene of PCOS. hsa-miR-1299, hsa-miR-122-5p, hsa-miR-199b- 3p, hsa-miR-199a-5p, hsa-miR-1307-3p, hsa-miR-96- 5p, and hsa-miR-548ar-3p are the 7 DEmiRNA that found both in PCOS son and PCOS mother which indicates the potential of these 7 DEmiRNAs as the factor gene for PCOS.
  1. Using the mice model, the researchers found that PCOS will cause transgenerational reproductive syndrome in the next generation. The lower testis weight is observed in these PCOS mice offspring F1 and F2 generation, even the only slight difference is observed in the F3 generation, the profound effect of androgenized and obesity still exists by decreasing the fertility rate and also lowering the testis weight. When the paternal is treated with androgen and the maternal side is also in obesity, transgenerationally metabolic dysfunction is also observed in the offspring and the syndrome like higher liver triglyceride is also observed in the F3 generation.

Possible Mechanism

The potential mechanism for this transgenerational inheritance is thought to be due to the differentially expressed miRNA and piRNA. This miRNA differential expression is passed down generational through the oocyte and affects the mtRNA in the oocyte and hence affects the offspring. The obesity of the mother can be treated as an environmental stress that alters the genetic expression within the embryo and leads them to experience trait change to accommodate this new environment.

Importance and Impact

The study of transgenerational transmission of reproductive and metabolic dysfunction across male offspring of polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) provides strong evidence concerning the impact of maternal health status on offspring beyond the generation immediately affected. No longer a disease of concern only to female reproductive health, PCOS is now shown to have long-term impacts on male offspring in terms of fertility, hormone level, and metabolic profile.

This research emphasizes that PCOS should not be perceived merely as an ovarian dysfunction in females but rather as an illness having systemic and inheritable implications. Evidence of disruption of metabolism and fertility in male offspring provides evidence of epigenetic mechanisms in transmitting responses to hormonal disruption in the mother. This evidence may shift clinic perception, potentially to earlier and family-centered therapy in managing PCOS.

Moreover, this research contributes to the expanding body of evidence supporting the influence of prenatal and perinatal conditions as the principal predictors of long-term health. This research also necessitates increased caution and tracking of those children born to persons afflicted with endocrine or metabolic disorders. Lastly, the research provides further depth into the transmission of disease and introduces such timely questions as prevention, diagnosis, and treatment procedures in both sexes and across generations.
